Unconventional error representations for Goal-Oriented Problems

This research aims to produce error indicators for the pre-asymptotical regime of highly unstable problems such as the Helmholtz equation and the convection-dominated diffusion problem in the context of p-GOA.

The classical approach provides an upper bound of the error in the Quantity of Interest (QoI) by the element-wise product of the direct and adjoint problems errors. This representation is used as error indicators to drive adaptive processes. However, for non-SPD problems, these indicators may not lead to convergent adaptive process due, for instance, to the pollution error in the Helmholtz case. We propose to compute the Riesz representation of the adjoint problem's error. Thus, we bound the error in the QoI by the element-wise product of the direct problem error and the Riesz representation. We studied the effect on a p-adaptive process applied to Helmholtz and convection-dominated diffusion problems. We observed an error stabilization phenomenon, and we were able to improve the adaptive process behavior.

Painless hp-adaptivity

We implement our method on top of Zander et al. hierarchical data-structure. Our main contribution resides in the adaptive strategy. For a given mesh, we aim at returning the coarsest discretization possible without overly increasing the error.

In this paper, we describe the algorithm and apply it to Symmetric Positive Definite problems (manufactured solutions for a Laplace equation). The adaptive algorithm is the following:

  1. Start with an initial coarse mesh
  2. Refine it (user-defined refinement, eg. global h-refinements and/or global p-refinements)
  3. Perform the coarsening step
  4. Return to step 1 until the mesh is fine enough and the error below the threshold
The unrefinement step proceeds as follows:
  1. Compute the solution
  2. Identify the removable basis functions
  3. Evaluate their contribution
  4. Remove the least contributing ones

To build the hp-coarsening step, we need a criteria to know if we should proceed to h- or p-unrefinements. For a given element,

  • If its contribution is small compare to the average of contributions then proceed to p-unrefinement,

  • if the contribution of its siblings are also small, then perform an h-unrefinement instead.

Generalized Golub-Kahan Bidigonalization

We are interested in saddle-point problems (linear elastic with MPC) from structural mechanics and provided by EDF. Historically, code_aster employs the double lagrangian approach to ensure that the saddle-point problem is SPD to use Cholesky factorization. However, this approach notably increased the system’s size, making it even more difficult to resolve via direct solvers. Hence, we need to find an adapted iterative solver for this situation. The idea proposed here explores the use of the generalized Golub-Kahan Bidiagonalization (GKB) iterative method on the augmented Lagrangian system to solve those problems.

The significant contribution is proof that for a sufficiently large augmentation parameter, the number of iterations of the GKB algorithm should be independent of the problem size. However, since we rely on eigenvalues' computation to determine the parameter values, we have to proceed experimentally.

To validate this result, we constructed a systematic study on test cases by increasing the difficulty both in terms of problem size and conditioning. We showed that, for with the parameter set to the 1-norm of the (1,1)-block, we obtain satisfactory convergence. The most challenging problem is the discretization of a containment building. We showed that even for this realistic industrial problem, we could achieve the convergence of the GKB within a few iterations. Finally, we showed that GKB scalability is limited by the (1,1)-block solver scalability.

Domain Decomposition with Inexact Direct Solvers

In the framework of a post-doc within the European Project EoCoE II, we are interested in linear elastic problems encountered in applications such as wind farms where structural mechanics are coupled with fluid dynamics. The need for scalable solvers is critical. I am investigating the use of Domain Decomposition Methods (DDM) coupled with MUMPS solver with the Block-Low Rank (BLR) feature as preconditioner for iterative methods.

We use FREEFEM++ interfaced with PETSc to call HPDDM, which, in turn, uses SLEPc to solve eigenvalue problems to build the coarse operator. HPDDM needs to perform several linear solves. The resolutions occur at three levels: First, on the fine grid, we solve the problem on a portion of the initial mesh where a single process is running independently. Then, still, on the fine grid, an eigenvalue solve is performed. Finally, we can build a matrix on the coarse grid based on the eigenvectors and eigenvalues on each subdomain. We chose to use MUMPS solver for all these three linear resolutions to investigate the use of the recent Block-Low Rank (BLR) method to improve the complexity of the overall solver.
